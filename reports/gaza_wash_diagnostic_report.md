# Gaza WASH Diagnostic Report

## Status

Diagnostic report status: source-gated working draft.

This report is sufficient for diagnostic framing, baseline comparison, public-health mechanism discussion, and evidence-gap documentation. It is not sufficient for definitive current Gaza household WASH prevalence estimates.

## Diagnostic question

What does the currently bound evidence indicate about Gaza WASH risk, baseline vulnerability, system stress, and remaining evidence gaps?

## Evidence gate

This report uses only bound or context-safe sources from the repository source-control layer. It does not promote blocked household assessment, local factsheet, original light-touch monitoring, RDNA table, or restricted IDP assessment claims into findings.

## Diagnostic findings

### Finding 1: Baseline WASH service indicators were high at the country level before full crisis-period field validation.

The derived JMP 2024 country-level table reports occupied Palestinian territory estimates of 98.771707% for at least basic drinking-water service, 80.642017% for safely managed drinking-water service, 99.395017% for at least basic sanitation, 73.511985% for safely managed sanitation, and 94.913292% for basic hygiene. These indicators establish a national baseline, not a Gaza-specific crisis condition.

Allowed inference: the diagnostic should frame current Gaza WASH collapse or stress against a pre-existing national service baseline, while stating clearly that the JMP figures are not current Gaza field measurements.

### Finding 2: Pre-crisis inequality data show structural dependence and geographic vulnerability relevant to Gaza.

The derived 2020 JMP inequalities table reports a Gaza regional tanker-truck drinking-water value of 81.290323% and a Gaza regional piped-into-dwelling drinking-water value of 3.322697%. This supports a diagnostic interpretation that Gaza had substantial pre-crisis dependence on non-networked or trucked drinking-water arrangements in the inequality workbook frame.

Allowed inference: this is a vulnerability baseline. It does not measure current displacement-site access, current liters per person per day, or current household water source distributions.

### Finding 3: Localized 2024 water-quality evidence supports a plausible fecal-contamination and waterborne-disease mechanism.

The Turkman et al. source is bound to the repository as localized water-quality evidence for Khan Younis and the Middle Area during May-June 2024. Repository page-binding notes identify source pages for metadata, methods, results, discussion, and limitations. The claim matrix limits this source to localized water-quality and mechanism language.

Allowed inference: the diagnostic can discuss microbial contamination and waterborne-disease risk mechanisms in sampled settings. It cannot generalize those findings as Gaza-wide prevalence.

### Finding 4: Political-economy constraints are central to WASH-system interpretation.

The Fayad et al. PLOS Water source is bound as sector-level political-economy context. Repository page-binding notes identify pages for the scoping-review purpose, methods, evidence map, results, and conclusions. The claim matrix limits this source to political-economy context and bars its use as current household WASH measurement.

Allowed inference: the diagnostic should treat Gaza WASH risk as a combined infrastructure, governance, financing, access, and political-economy problem. Technical repair alone should not be framed as a sufficient explanatory or intervention model.

### Finding 5: Health-care-facility WASH baselines show facility-system vulnerabilities even before Gaza-specific crisis assessment.

The derived JMP WinHCF 2023 table reports occupied Palestinian territory HCF estimates of 93.3% for basic water service, 86.7% for basic hygiene service, 57.5% for basic waste-management service, and 33.4% for basic environmental-cleaning service. Hospital-specific entries include 84.9% basic water service and 52.8% basic environmental-cleaning service.

Allowed inference: HCF WASH should be included in the diagnostic risk frame, especially for infection prevention and continuity of care. These figures are country-level facility estimates, not Gaza-specific crisis-damage measurements.

### Finding 6: Official OCHA situation reports can support time-stamped crisis-context language but not replace missing monitoring reports.

The April 17 and May 15 2026 OCHA situation reports are bound as official time-stamped context sources. The repository gate explicitly says these rows are not replacements for the original LTM Round 3 or Round 4 sources and not replacements for RDNA table evidence.

Allowed inference: these official reports may be used to describe time-stamped crisis context. They cannot be used to reconstruct original LTM trends or RDNA damage tables.

## Diagnostic conclusion

The evidence base is sufficient for a diagnostic report that compares national and pre-crisis WASH baselines against localized water-quality risk, political-economy constraints, health-care-facility baseline vulnerabilities, and official time-stamped crisis context. The evidence base is not sufficient for definitive current Gaza household prevalence estimates or local factsheet-based case findings.

The strongest report framing is therefore: baseline-to-crisis diagnostic with explicit evidence gaps. The report should not be framed as a final epidemiologic assessment.
