# Journal Targeting

## Current manuscript type

Working manuscript: rapid secondary-data public-health systems analysis.

Core contribution: a reproducible WASH--health priority index for Gaza using open humanitarian data.

Current strength: public-health systems fit, WASH-health risk framing, open-data reproducibility, humanitarian context.

Current weakness: first-pass data are aggregate or semi-aggregate; WHO, WASH Cluster, and HDX validation layers remain pending.

## Primary target

### 1. Conflict and Health

Best fit.

Rationale:

- Focuses on public-health impacts and responses related to armed conflict, humanitarian crises, and forced migration.
- Explicitly includes infectious disease prevention and control, health systems, ethics, and public health in conflicts and humanitarian crises.
- The manuscript's strongest identity is conflict-affected public-health prioritization, not generic WASH engineering.

Submission framing:

- Original research / methodology-informed public-health systems analysis.
- Emphasize armed conflict, displacement, WASH-health convergence, health-system stress, and transparent prioritization.

Risk:

- Needs stronger validation layer before submission.
- Editors may reject if the manuscript reads as descriptive humanitarian reporting rather than research.

Required before submission:

- Add WHO/Health Cluster facility-functionality layer if accessible.
- Add HDX boundary or facility geodata layer if accessible.
- Improve discussion of method transferability to other conflict settings.

## Secondary targets

### 2. PLOS Water

Strong fit if the manuscript is framed around WASH, water systems, access, equity, and computational/secondary-data prioritization.

Rationale:

- Scope includes water, sanitation and hygiene, infrastructure, policy, equity, access, and computational work.
- Good match if we strengthen the WASH-specific index and geospatial method.

Risk:

- Less conflict/public-health-system specific than Conflict and Health.
- Needs clear WASH contribution beyond Gaza description.

### 3. PLOS Global Public Health

Strong fit if the manuscript is framed around global health inequity, conflict/displacement, evidence use, public-health governance, and methodological transparency.

Rationale:

- Scope includes humanitarian aid, conflict/displacement, environmental and planetary health, evidence use and policy, infectious diseases, and global health delivery.
- Accepts methodologically rigorous research and tools/databases when utility, validation, and availability are demonstrated.

Risk:

- Higher burden to show methodological rigor, data availability, and generalizable contribution.

### 4. BMC Public Health

Viable broad backup target.

Rationale:

- Scope includes disaster and emergency preparedness and response, environmental health, global health and human rights, health systems, infectious disease epidemiology, and public health informatics.

Risk:

- Broader journal; Gaza WASH index may be desk-rejected if contribution is too narrow or too preliminary.
- Needs stronger epidemiologic or methods contribution.

## Not first-line yet

High prestige but not the best first submission in the manuscript's current state.

Reason:

- The paper needs stronger empirical depth, policy argument, and validation before targeting a higher-bar global health outlet.

### General environmental health journals

Possible later, but current framing is humanitarian public health rather than exposure science.

## Gate criteria before first submission

- All source claims must be in the extraction table.
- All manuscript claims must be supported by citations.
- Tables must compile cleanly.
- Index scoring must be reproducible from CSV files.
- WHO/HDX/WASH validation layers should be attempted and documented, even if incomplete.
- Discussion must state why the method is transferable beyond Gaza.
- Limitations must clearly distinguish risk signals from individual-level outcomes.

## Positioning sentence

This manuscript presents a transparent, reproducible, open-data WASH--health priority index for conflict-affected settings, using Gaza as a case study to show how fragmented humanitarian reporting can be converted into auditable public-health decision support.
